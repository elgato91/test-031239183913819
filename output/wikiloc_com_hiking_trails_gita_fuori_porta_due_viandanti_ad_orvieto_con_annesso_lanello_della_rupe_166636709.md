## Itinerary description

 Orvieto is built on a high pedestal of tuff with sheer sides, colored between brown and yellowish, shaped like a ship, dominating the vineyards. A steep road cuts halfway up the slopes, rising from the plain to the town, all suspended in the air.

### DUOMO

 The "Golden Lily" of Cathedrals As a symbol of the city of Orvieto itself, the Duomo is one of the most beautiful cathedrals in Italy and the world. Construction began in 1290, strongly desired by both the Church and the Municipality, with the aim of creating a single, grand cathedral for the city to replace the two dilapidated churches that previously stood on the beautiful square (the episcopal church of S. Maria, in rather poor condition, and the parish church of S. Costanzo, officiated by canons who owned houses and shops adjacent to the attached cloister). Therefore, we must exclude the suggestive theory that indicates the Miracle of Bolsena as the propitious event for the construction. The Duomo presents itself as a magnificent construction that incorporates different architectural styles. In particular, it can be defined as a remarkable example of balance and mixtures of Gothic styles (especially for the facade with its slender spires) and Romanesque, even if the cathedral maintains its own unique peculiarities. Many people and workers participated in the different phases of design and construction of the Duomo.

### Albornoz Fortress

 The construction of the Albornoz fortress within the city began on September 25, 1364, despite the fact that the city was already a natural stronghold. Orvieto can boast the absence of walls, precisely because of the peculiarity of its elevated position on the tuff cliff. The construction served as a defense against external attacks, but above all to show the presence of central power and the strength of the Pope. The project was probably entrusted to Ugolino di Montemarte (c. 1320 - 1388), a landmark architect, count, and above all, a Guelph.

### St. Patrick's Well

 "A true masterpiece of Renaissance engineering." It was 1527 when Pope Clement VII took refuge in Orvieto during the Sack of Rome. He commissioned Antonio da Sangallo the Younger to build the well, which was intended to supply water in case the city of Orvieto was besieged. To think it's about 500 years old and a fantastic feat of engineering—one side goes down and the other goes up without ever meeting... marvelous!
